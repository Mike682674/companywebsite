How is it Different from Point-to-Point Cabling?

Point-to-point cabling can get very disorganized and requires a lot of maintenance. If you want a neat and clean system, then structured cabling is the way to go. Since jumper fiber cables are used in point-to-point cabling, you cannot patch panels to the Main Distribution Area. As a result, you can only run the cables to and from the equipment. It is not possible for large enterprises, where there are tons of equipment across the floor. On the other hand, if you need to connect different hardware to your primary data center, Structured Cabling can offer a more extensive facility.

Components of a Network Defined by Structured Cabling

Structured system cabling has six components, according to ANSI/TIA-568-C.0 (Generic Telecommunications Cabling for Customer Premises) and ANSI/TIA-568-C.1 (Commercial Building Telecommunications Cabling Standard). The ANSI/TIA-568-C.0 (Generic Telecommunications Cabling for Customer Premises) standard is maintained for generic infrastructure. The ANSI/TIA-568-C.1 (Commercial Building Telecommunications Cabling Standard) is maintained for commercial building infrastructure.

Entrance Facilities (EF)

Your entrance facilities consist of cables, connecting hardware, network demarcation points, and other equipment. EF helps connect to the private network cable layout or the access provider (AP). Also, it helps build the cabling connection between the inside building cabling and the outside plant. That is the part of your Structured System cabling, where the telecom company's wiring ends, and your company's wiring begins. You can connect the provider's cabling to your private cabling using the demarcation points, connecting hardware, or any other equipment. All of these are available in entrance facilities.

Equipment Room (ER)

You need a more environmentally controlled centralized space for telecommunications equipment. It is more complex than a typical telecommunications room (TR) or telecommunications enclosure (TE). Having it environmentally controlled means you can modify and adapt the features to your physical workplace to enhance business effectiveness. It also consists of the main cross-connect (MC), intermediate cross-connects (IC), and horizontal cross-connects (HC).

Telecommunications Room (TR) and Telecommunications Enclosure (TE)

A telecommunications room helps connect the hardware to the horizontal terminals and backbone cables. That includes nay patch cords or jumpers as well. In an FTTE structured cabling installation, the backbone fiber runs through the telecommunications room (TR), starting from the entrance facility. Similarly, a telecommunications enclosure (TE) acts as a secondary telecommunications room (TR) in some instances. You can say that the TE can work as a more miniature TR that helps consolidate, distribute, and offer a point of network cable termination nearby. This way, you can establish the final link to your desktop via a fiber, UTP cable, or even wireless connection.

Horizontal Cabling

The physical cabling and terminating hardware that helps transmit data and voice signals between the Work Area Outlets and TR is called Horizontal Cabling. The maximum length of the horizontal cable can extend up to 90 meters or 295 ft. As you can see, this long network cable can come in handy. Horizontal cabling is also considered as cabling subsystem 1. Cabling subsystem 2 is the backbone cabling between the horizontal and intermediate cross-connect. There is a cabling subsystem 3 that connects between an intermediate cross-connect and the main cross-connect. The horizontal cabling subsystem 1 stretches from your primary work area's telecommunication information outlet to the TR or TE. It comprises a horizontal cable, some mechanical terminations, patch cords, and jumpers.

Work Area (WA)

The Work area of WA components expands from the telecommunication connector end of the horizontal cabling system to the Work Area equipment. You would need at least two permanent telecommunications outlets for each work area. The multiuser telecommunications outlet assembly (MUTOA) is a part of the WA. Now, when planning horizontal cabling, what is the maximum allowable distance that can be used? Now ideally, the answer is 76 meters or 250 feet. That is why your telecommunication room needs to be on the same floor as the work area.

Backbone Cabling

You need backbone cabling to interconnect between the other structured cabling components of the Structured wiring system. A structured cable installation usually happens from floor to floor or between the IT rooms on the same floor.

What does backbone cabling consist of?

The components, which the backbone cabling system helps interconnect, include the entrance facilities, the equipment rooms, the access provider (AP) spaces, and the telecommunications rooms.

  • Backbone cables
  • Intermediate connects
  • Main cross-connects
  • Mechanical network cable termination
  • Patch cords or jumpers There are three types of backbone cabling:
  • Coaxial
  • Twisted pair (shielded or unshielded)
  • Fiber optic
